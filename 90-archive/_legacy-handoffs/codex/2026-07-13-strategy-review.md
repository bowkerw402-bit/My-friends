# Codex adversarial review: triggered outreach strategies

Date: 14 July 2026  
Scope: local review of `Business/govt-supplier-evidence-desk/10-triggered-outreach-strategy.md` and `APLakeside/GTM-TRIGGERED-OUTREACH.md`  
Evidence used: the stored prospect ledgers, personalised-page records, outreach payloads, delivery registers and APLakeside operating documents. No new web research was performed.

## 1. Government Supplier Truth and Evidence Desk

### A. ATTACK

#### The strategy promotes an attractive theory into a proven primary niche without evidence

The central shift from segment targeting to live triggers is directionally right. The document then overreaches by calling newly onboarded panel suppliers the primary niche. Our stored research does not validate that choice.

The 30-prospect batch and 20-prospect batch contain 50 research rows but only 46 unique firms because Exhale People, Coolamon Advisors, Sustineo and Testgrid were reconsidered in batch 2. In those 50 rows, the local evidence supports 11 unambiguous panel or standing-offer holders: Griffin Legal, Morpht, ConceptSix, RD Consulting, Oxide Interactive, Gurugun, 1LM, Define Potential, Freebody Cogent, Bluebird Consultants and Annex Digital. Strategic Development Group is a possible twelfth because its Finance profile and MAS categories point to an arrangement, but the stored record does not state the membership cleanly enough to count it as confirmed.

That is about 22% of the 50 research rows, or 24% of the 46 unique firms. More importantly, zero of those firms was verified in our work as a newly added supplier with a recent join date. The research proves that panel membership exists in the market. It does not prove that a usable stream of newly onboarded 5 to 50 person suppliers can be found every week, that the addition date is public, or that joining creates an immediate unmanaged evidence problem.

The document also treats panel entry as if it always increases bid activity. A panel can create eligibility without invitations, and a supplier may already have mature bid operations before joining. "The clock just started" is rhetoric, not an observed fact. T1 should be a hypothesis to test, not the primary niche.

#### Panel expiry is not automatically a buying trigger

T2 assumes a panel approaching expiry means the individual supplier is about to requalify. Some arrangements are extended, replaced, refreshed in tranches or closed without an immediate supplier action. The arrangement expiry is not necessarily the supplier's submission deadline. The trigger is usable only when the public source establishes an actual refresh, application window, extension request or supplier action date.

Interaction Consulting is the best warning. Its public CSIRO date had passed, but our page correctly said it might have renewed or might need checking. The strategy must preserve that uncertainty. A date on a website is a verification prompt, not proof of a live requalification burden.

#### Multi-panel membership is a fit signal, not a timing signal

T3 identifies firms likely to have more reusable evidence. It does not establish present buying intent. Griffin Legal's 12 panel appointments make it a strong account, but without a refresh, expiry, bid hire, certificate event or current procurement it is still ordinary cold outreach. The strategy currently mixes segmentation signals and event triggers in one table.

It needs three separate fields:

| Field | What it means | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Fit | The account plausibly has recurring evidence work | multiple panels, many agencies, frequent open awards |
| Trigger | A dated event may have changed the workload now | actual panel refresh, bid-role hire, accreditation renewal |
| Proof gap | A specific public record can be checked | stale panel date, certificate identifier absent, old capability PDF |

Contact only when fit is strong and either a real trigger or a high-consequence proof gap is present.

#### The free snapshot is mostly a repackaging of what batches 1 and 2 already did

The claim that the new snapshot changes the first touch is overstated. Batch 1 already led with a sourced observation. Next Peak's page named three ISO logos without certificate numbers or expiry dates. Interaction Consulting's page named the CSIRO panel date that had passed. Work Science's email led with NATA and ISO 45001. Morpht's led with its official GovCMS profile. Those are already snapshots in substance.

The proposed three-clause opener is sharper because it joins a dated trigger to the gap, but the free label does not create a new product. If the recipient still receives a cold note from an unknown sender that identifies a public omission and asks for a call, the experience is materially similar. Calling it free is cosmetic unless the recipient receives a genuinely useful, bounded artefact without an immediate sales cliff.

Batch 2 was weaker than batch 1 in an important way. Many pages said the public website did not show an internal evidence owner or review date. Firms are not expected to publish their internal owners or review controls on a marketing site. That absence does not prove a gap. It risks making the research look formulaic rather than insightful. The new strategy should explicitly ban "not visible publicly" as a gap unless the item is one a buyer would reasonably expect to verify publicly, such as a certificate status, panel date, accreditation scope or published capability statement date.

#### What probably blocked replies was not just lack of timing

The local work points to several blockers that the strategy understates:

1. Many messages went to `info@`, `admin@`, `sales@` or another functional inbox even when the page named a director. That breaks the person-to-person premise.
2. The sender and offer have no demonstrated case study, referral proof or recognised specialist authority yet. A precise observation does not by itself solve the trust gap.
3. "Evidence library," "evidence cards" and "source ownership" are unfamiliar solution language. The pain may be real while the category remains unclear.
4. The offer moved rapidly from observation to a call and A$750. The recipient had no proof of saved time or a sample deliverable from a real client.
5. The attached PDF made a cold first touch heavier. It asks the recipient to trust an attachment from an unknown sender and can create filtering or security friction.
6. Several gaps were inferred from website absence rather than demonstrated operational failure.
7. Award count is a lagging signal. Winning several contracts does not tell us who writes tenders, whether evidence retrieval is painful, or whether the firm is currently bidding.
8. No controlled send log establishes that all "33 emails" were delivered exposures. The local registers distinguish Gmail drafts, form-only staged messages and earlier seed drafts. A draft or staged form is not a sent email, and same-day silence is not a reply-rate baseline.

The `<5% baseline` and `10 to 20% expected reply rate` should therefore be removed until sent, delivered and elapsed-time data are reconciled. Fifteen sends is also too small for a 20% scale decision: three replies would trigger scaling and two would not.

#### Some trigger claims are too strong

- T4 is useful only after a live JAS-ANZ procedure is proven. A register miss may be an entity-name, certification-body or scope mismatch. It should not be scored High until the lookup workflow is reliable.
- T5 combines DISP, JOSCAR and ISO into one implied requirement stack. Requirements vary by prime, work package and information classification. Self-declaration signals exposure to assurance, not that every listed standard is required "just to be considered."
- T6 is strong for workload and names a function, but it may mean the firm is solving the problem internally and has no appetite for an external service. It is a two-sided signal.
- "Zero expired-evidence submission mistakes" is an outcome promise the service cannot guarantee. The offer can reduce the risk of stale evidence; it cannot promise zero mistakes.
- The A$250 monthly monitoring promise is premature when the monitored sources include semi-manual pages, unreliable automation and records that are not public.

#### The weekly rhythm will not survive Will's real week as written

The claimed 10 to 15 minutes per snapshot is inconsistent with our execution. Verifying entity identity, size, decision-maker role, contact route, panel status, certificate language and source wording took materially longer. The hard part was not writing three clauses. It was reaching a confidence level safe enough to put Will's name on them.

The schedule also hides work: source archiving, duplicate checks, suppression checks, address and signature control, attachment generation, draft readback, follow-up tracking and reconciling contacts across cohorts. Will is running multiple ventures and has already expanded batches faster than follow-up records can mature. Four separate weekly blocks plus referral work will fragment rather than focus the effort.

Replace the rhythm with one 90-minute trigger sprint per fortnight, capped at two fully verified targets, one batch review by Will, and a separate 20-minute follow-up block. Review after eight genuine triggered sends, not after an assumed weekly habit.

The five strongest re-target candidates from the 50-row research set are:

2. Griffin Legal. Twelve panel appointments create the highest known evidence surface. Target only after identifying the government-team operations or bid owner and one current refresh or expiry.
3. Gurugun. Several Commonwealth panels plus Supply Nation create multiple verifiable record types and a stronger expiry/currency story than a generic capability gap.

#### Triggers the strategy missed

- Accreditation scope or registration change, not just ISO: NATA scope, RTO registration, SOC 2 report period, Supply Nation status and JOSCAR renewal.
- A capability statement or supplier price book newly published, materially updated or visibly stale.
- A named bid coordinator joining, leaving or being recruited. Strategic Development Group already showed dedicated bid coordination.
- Acquisition, merger or rapid office expansion. This creates a genuine consolidation problem for claims, CVs and case studies.
- A new service line or new government supplier profile. This changes the reusable claim set even without a new panel.
- A public panel record with a date already passed, as with Interaction Consulting. Phrase it as a check, never as proof of lapse.
- A sudden cross-agency award surge. This is not a live trigger by itself, but combined with a bid hire or profile update it becomes useful.

#### Channel ranking should change based on what execution taught us

The document's universal LinkedIn-first ranking is untested. LinkedIn may expose a named person, but a connection request from a stranger who has audited the firm's certificates can still feel invasive, and reliable access to current role data is manual.

A more defensible ranking is conditional:

1. Warm referral from a certifier, bid writer, panel adviser or existing client.
2. Plain-text email to a verified named work address, with one source link and no attachment on first touch.
3. LinkedIn after a genuine shared context, event, referral or visible post by the recipient.
4. Functional inbox only when it is the firm's explicit tender, proposal or procurement route.
5. Cold PDF attachment or generic `info@` inbox as a last resort, not the default.

The Gmail mechanics matter operationally. Attachment draft creation produced duplicate copies, some returned draft IDs were not readable, and attachment drafts could not be safely edited in place. We had to create replacements, read them back, verify account and attachment, then trash the superseded draft. That is manageable for an approved send-day batch, but it is too fragile for a high-frequency first-touch factory. Plain text first touch is simpler, safer and easier to audit. Send the PDF only after a reply or explicit request.

### C. VERDICT

#### MUST change before Will operates from it

1. Recast new panel suppliers from "PRIMARY" to an unvalidated trigger hypothesis.
2. Separate fit, trigger and proof gap. Multi-panel status is fit, not timing.
3. Remove the claim that 33 emails establish a `<5%` baseline until sent, delivered, elapsed and replied records are reconciled.
4. State that none of the 50 rows was verified as a newly added panel supplier.
5. Ban internal-control inferences based only on what a marketing website does not display.
6. Remove "zero expired-evidence submission mistakes" and qualify the DISP/ISO requirement language.
7. Make first touch plain text with no PDF attachment; reserve PDFs for replies or requested information.
8. Replace the 10 to 15 minute snapshot assumption and fragmented weekly schedule with a smaller timed pilot.
9. Add a named-recipient gate. A director's name on a page does not fix delivery to a generic inbox.

#### Nice to have

1. Build a live trigger ledger with separate confidence scores for entity, event, recipient and gap.
2. Create a verified JAS-ANZ lookup procedure with entity-name variations and a screenshot record.
3. Test a genuinely useful micro-deliverable, such as a two-row expiry table, against a plain three-clause note.
4. Build the referral-partner track, but only after a sample deliverable and clear referral proposition exist.
5. Track bounce, routing, wrong-person, reply, qualified-pain and payment separately.

#### Solid as-is

1. The strategic preference for observable timing over undifferentiated volume.
2. The cautious "could not confirm" language and two-source principle.
3. The rejection of guarantees, classified work and auto-submission.
4. The idea that recurring panel and accreditation records can support a monitoring product, provided it remains explicitly unvalidated.
5. The insistence on low-volume, evidence-led outreach rather than mass mail.

## 2. APLakeside

### A. ATTACK

#### The procurement framing contains a serious contradiction

The strategy says the sub-A$10k price is "how a risk-aware buyer keeps a defensible spend out of procurement-scrutiny territory" and instructs Will to say that plainly. This must not be used.

The existing `CLAUDE OBSIDIAN/APLakeside/13 - Client Procurement and Outreach Pack.md` says the opposite: do not claim an EL1 can approve a booking, that no purchase order or procurement process is required, or that the service is fully acquittable. Thresholds are not delegations. Agency-specific financial delegations, value-for-money requirements, conflicts, quote requirements and internal controls still apply below A$10,000.

"Out of procurement-scrutiny territory" sounds like structuring spend to avoid scrutiny. It is commercially and reputationally dangerous in public-sector outreach. The safe claim is that the packages are modest, clearly scoped and supported by a quote, agreement, insurance and delivery records for assessment through the agency's normal process. Nothing more.

The same problem affects the claim that paperwork makes the spend capability development. Documentation can evidence what was delivered. It cannot reclassify a boat, venue, catering and facilitation package by assertion. The content, facilitator capability, learning objectives, buyer approval and actual delivery must support the description. Per-attendee "certificates" may make this worse if they look like an accredited credential when none exists. Use attendance and development records, not certificates, unless their non-accredited status is unmistakable.

#### The census action-plan trigger is account-level, not team-level

An agency census plan does not tell us which branch needs APLakeside, which EL1 owns the action, which team is in Canberra, which budget can fund it, or whether an external facilitated day is an approved response. Saying "your plan" to a random EL1 is technically true at agency level but personally irrelevant.

The document's sample line overclaims by saying the development record can be reported directly against the commitment. That is only true if the agency's action owner accepts the activity as part of the plan. A vendor cannot predeclare reportability.

T1 is therefore an account-context signal. It becomes actionable only when paired with a team-level event and an accountable recipient, for example a newly appointed branch leader, a newly formed branch, a documented merger, or a public role responsible for the named action.

#### The May 2025 MoG trigger is stale

The cited May and June 2025 changes are about 14 months old. "I saw [function] moved" now reads like delayed prospecting unless there is current evidence that integration remains active: a 2026 implementation update, new structure, current recruitment, new branch head, changed corporate plan or active transition program.

The broad inference "every moved function = a merged team, two cultures, two sets of norms" is also invented. A function can move administratively with staff intact, be absorbed without a merger, or have completed integration months ago. AAO publication identifies a portfolio change, not team dysfunction or a need for a facilitated day.

The weekly AAO check is low-yield busywork. AAOs are episodic, not a meaningful every-Monday lead source. Check when a new AAO is announced or quarterly, then require a current named-team corroboration.

#### The austerity reframe risks euphemism and political exposure

Avoiding "reward" and "junket" language is sensible. Renaming an offsite a "structured capability day" does not make it defensible if the substance still looks like hospitality. The strategy needs to protect truth, not just vocabulary.

The strongest defence is a real agenda, named learning or operating objective, qualified facilitation, tangible team outputs, proportionate catering and a clear business reason. If those are not present, the positioning sheet and paperwork are cosmetic.

The blanket ban on agencies with live redundancy rounds is prudent in tone but hard to operate reliably. News coverage may lag, rounds may affect one function rather than the whole agency, and a monthly blacklist can become stale. Treat redundancy as a high-risk review flag requiring current confirmation, not an automatically complete market map.

#### New-leader intent is plausible but still untested

A new EL1 or EL2 may want an early team intervention, but may also have no discretionary budget, be under onboarding pressure, inherit an existing supplier, or avoid visible discretionary spending in the first 90 days. A vacancy close is not an appointment, and a LinkedIn update does not prove team need.

The existing APLakeside research also shows a routing problem: many public emails belong to recruitment contact officers whose exact substantive roles were unclear. They are not sales leads merely because their address is public. The new strategy should prohibit using a vacancy contact address unless the person is independently verified as the relevant team leader or explicitly responsible for the transition.

#### The channel and metric assumptions are not earned

The 15% reply target and 20-touch decision rule are hypotheses presented with too much confidence. Twenty messages cannot distinguish a bad trigger from poor routing, weak sender trust, deliverability, seasonality or procurement sensitivity.

The strategy also ignores the actual channel split. APLakeside sending has been staged in Gmail but sent from Titan using `williambowker@aplakeside.com.au`. That custom-domain route is preferable to a consumer Gmail identity, but sender authentication, delivery, bounce and reply records need to be measured before declaring a message problem or a trigger success. LinkedIn and IPAA are not automatically warmer: an event is warm only when Will actually met the person or can reference a genuine shared interaction.

#### The weekly rhythm will decay

The plan requires APSJobs monitoring, six-week reminders, LinkedIn confirmation, gazette checks, redundancy monitoring, drafting, annual census extraction, quarterly IPAA attendance and referral touches. The existing workspace already contains multiple large Titan draft batches and follow-up obligations. Adding more watchers without one authoritative CRM will create duplicate targeting and stale reminders.

The realistic cadence is one weekly 60-minute qualification block, capped at two contact-ready prospects. AAO review should be event-driven or quarterly. Census plans should be processed once per annual release and revisited only when a team-level trigger appears. IPAA attendance is a deliberate quarterly business-development activity, not a recurring checklist item.

### B. EXTEND

#### Build the first ten targets with a two-level trigger, not a list of agency names

Use this construction method:

1. Start with the four locally identified 2025 action-plan accounts: PM&C, ABS, Industry/Science/Resources and the Commonwealth Ombudsman.
2. Extract the exact commitment, the published delivery period and any named accountable group. If the plan names no owner, route only to the agency's publicly verified People, Capability, Workforce or Transformation executive. Do not target arbitrary operational EL1s from the plan alone.
3. For each agency, find one independently public team-level event from the last 120 days: a named new leader, newly established branch, current structural integration, clustered hiring under one leader, or a public event where the leader discussed the issue.
4. Create two slots per agency: one central action owner and one team-level buyer with the second trigger. That produces eight candidates, but a slot remains empty if no named person and official work route can be verified.
5. Fill the final two slots from current new-leader appointments confirmed by both Gazette or APSJobs and a current organisation or professional profile. A vacancy contact alone does not qualify.
6. Score every candidate on four gates: current trigger date, direct relevance to their team, verified authority or ownership, and legitimate contact route. Require all four. Do not backfill weak names just to reach ten.

The list should look like this before any drafting:

| Slot | Account | Recipient type | Required second proof |
|---:|---|---|---|
| 1 | PM&C | published census-action owner | exact action and current ownership |
| 2 | PM&C | named branch leader | team event within 120 days |
| 3 | ABS | published census-action owner | exact action and current ownership |
| 4 | ABS | named branch leader | team event within 120 days |
| 5 | Industry/Science/Resources | published census-action owner | exact action and current ownership |
| 6 | Industry/Science/Resources | named branch leader | team event within 120 days |
| 7 | Commonwealth Ombudsman | published census-action owner | exact action and current ownership |
| 8 | Commonwealth Ombudsman | named branch leader | team event within 120 days |
| 9 | APS or ACT agency | confirmed new EL1/EL2 | appointment plus current team remit |
| 10 | APS or ACT agency | confirmed new EL1/EL2 | appointment plus current team remit |

This is deliberately a construction template rather than ten invented names. The local evidence does not contain ten currently verified Commonwealth action owners, and pretending otherwise would repeat the routing problem.

#### Improve the trigger hierarchy

Use this order:

1. Warm event or referral plus a verified team change.
2. Confirmed new leader plus a current team formation, merger or clustered hiring signal.
3. Census plan plus a current team-level trigger and named owner.
4. Fresh MoG change plus a current named implementation team.
5. Agency-wide plan alone: nurture/content audience, not direct sales trigger.
6. Old MoG event alone: reject.

#### Make the offer substantively procurement-safe

- Replace "certificate" with a plainly non-accredited attendance and development record.
- Publish a sample agenda with objectives, facilitated exercises, outputs and follow-through.
- State facilitator credentials accurately and do not imply accredited training.
- Provide an internal justification template that says the buyer must adapt and approve it. Never claim the spend is reportable against a census plan.
- Keep the procurement language from the existing pack: normal agency assessment still applies.
- Consider a lower-friction first product without the boat component, or a short paid planning workshop, if buyers say the hospitality optics block the full package. That is a test, not an assumed pivot.

#### Channel ranking after the actual execution experience

1. Genuine referral or a real conversation at IPAA or another Canberra event.
2. Named work email from the authenticated `aplakeside.com.au` sender, plain text, no unsolicited attachment.
3. LinkedIn follow-up after a real shared context or after the recipient has publicly discussed the trigger.
4. Official general route asking who owns team capability or transition, without pitching a named individual through a recruitment address.
5. Cold email to a recruitment contact or broad executive list: reject unless role relevance is independently verified.

Gmail should remain a staging and review surface if useful, but Titan is the sending system for this venture. Maintain one send ledger across both so staged Gmail copies are never counted as sends and Titan sends are not duplicated.

#### MUST change before Will operates from it

1. Delete every claim that sub-A$10k means EL1 approval, less scrutiny or avoidance of procurement process.
2. Remove the instruction to "say it plainly" and restore the procurement wording from the existing outreach pack.
3. Treat census plans as account context, not team-level need. Require a second current trigger and named owner.
4. Retire the May 2025 MoG event as a live trigger unless current implementation evidence exists.
5. Stop claiming the capability record is automatically reportable against an agency action plan.
6. Replace per-attendee certificates with accurately labelled non-accredited records.

9. Reduce the monitoring cadence and maintain one authoritative prospect and suppression ledger.

#### Nice to have

1. Build the first-ten table using the two-level method above, leaving slots blank when evidence is insufficient.
2. Produce a real sample agenda and internal justification template for buyer review.
3. Test whether a facilitation-first, lower-hospitality entry product gets through procurement optics more easily.
4. Add a 120-day freshness rule to every event trigger.
5. Track wrong-person routing separately from no reply.

#### Solid as-is

1. The avoidance of opportunistic outreach during active redundancy rounds, subject to current verification.
2. The rule against implying dysfunction or using inferred private facts.
3. The preference for a small number of publicly warranted touches over broad executive blasting.
4. The new-leader and genuinely fresh team-formation hypotheses, when appointments and remit are verified.
5. The emphasis on useful leave-behinds, clear paperwork and tangible post-session outputs.
6. The decision to treat consulting firms and defence primes as referral-led until a credible warm route exists.

## Bottom line across both ventures

Claude's common insight is good: observable timing should beat undifferentiated lists. The common failure is treating an account-level public fact as if it proves a team-level buying moment. Neither panel membership nor a census plan does that by itself.

The operating rule for both ventures should be:

> Strong fit + current dated event + named accountable person + legitimate route + one defensible gap. If any of those is missing, nurture or hold rather than manufacture urgency.

That rule will produce fewer drafts. It will also make the triggered strategy meaningfully different from the batches already run.
